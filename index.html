Types of Smoking

Know your enemy

Tobacco products include cigarettes, e-cigarettes, heated tobacco, hookah, and smokeless tobacco. Each carries its own dangers.

🚬

Cigarettes

  • Tobacco burned directly
  • Highest toxic exposure
  • Causes lung cancer & COPD
EXTREME RISK
💨

Vape (E-Cigarettes)

  • Heated e-liquid aerosol
  • Nicotine addiction
  • Respiratory & cardiovascular effects
HIGH RISK
🫧

Hookah (Shisha)

  • Flavored tobacco, long sessions
  • High smoke exposure
  • Increased cancer risk
HIGH RISK
🔥

IQOS (Heated Tobacco)

  • Tobacco heated, not burned
  • Lower emissions than cigarettes
  • Still increases COPD & heart disease risk
MODERATE RISK
🌫️

Passive Smoking

  • Secondhand smoke exposure
  • Harmful even to non-smokers
  • Both active and passive smoking negatively affect health
INVOLUNTARY RISK
Psychology

Why do people smoke?

Understanding the psychological causes is the first step to breaking free.

Stress Relief

Used to reduce stress and anxiety, creating a false sense of calm.

Social Influence

Peer pressure and influence from friends and social groups.

Habit & Addiction

Nicotine creates dependence and cravings. Becomes part of daily routines.

Emotional Coping

Used to deal with sadness, anger, or boredom. A temporary escape.

Body Damage

How smoking destroys your body

7,000 harmful chemicals in tobacco smoke attack every organ system.

☠️

Toxic Chemicals

  • CO → reduced oxygen delivery (hypoxia)
  • Tar → airway damage
  • Nicotine → addiction, ↑ heart rate & blood pressure
🔬

Oxidative Stress

  • Free radicals damage DNA and cells
  • Chronic inflammation injures lung tissue
  • Impaired repair mechanisms
🫁

Respiratory Damage

  • Destruction of cilia
  • Mucus accumulation & infections
  • COPD: chronic bronchitis & emphysema

Organ Harm & Clinical Outcomes

❤️

Cardiovascular

  • Endothelial dysfunction
  • Accelerated atherosclerosis
  • Coronary artery disease (CAD)
  • Myocardial infarction (heart attack)
  • Peripheral arterial disease
🫁

Pulmonary

  • COPD (chronic bronchitis & emphysema)
  • Lung cancer
  • Recurrent respiratory infections
  • Progressive decline in lung function
  • Popcorn Lung (vaping)
🧬

Carcinogenesis

  • DNA mutations caused by carcinogens
  • Impaired apoptosis and cell repair
  • Multi-organ damage
⚡

Systemic Effects

  • Chronic tissue hypoxia
  • Immune suppression
  • Delayed wound healing
  • Reduced quality of life

Recovery Timeline

Your body starts healing immediately

From the first 20 minutes to 15 years — here's the remarkable timeline of recovery after you quit.

Immediate

20 Minutes

Heart rate and blood pressure begin to normalize.

Immediate

12 Hours

Carbon monoxide is cleared. Oxygen levels return to normal.

Short-term

2 Days

Sense of smell and taste begin to improve.

Short-term

2 Weeks

Circulation improves. Endothelial function begins recovering. Walking becomes easier.

Medium-term

1–9 Months

Lung function improves ~10%. Cilia recover. Cough and shortness of breath decrease. Fewer respiratory infections.

Long-term

1 Year

Coronary heart disease risk drops by 50%. A major milestone!

Long-term

5 Years

Risk of stroke and several cancers decreases significantly.

Long-term

10 Years

Lung cancer risk drops by ~50%. Other cancer risks decrease too.

Long-term

15 Years

Coronary artery disease risk approaches that of a non-smoker. You made it.

✅ Reversible Damage

  • Cardiovascular function improves
  • Blood oxygen normalizes
  • Ciliary activity recovers

⚠️ Irreversible Damage

  • Emphysema (permanent alveolar destruction)
  • Airway fibrosis & scarring
  • Some DNA mutations & epigenetic changes persist
Treatment

There is a way out

Smoking cessation is most successful when behavioral support is combined with evidence-based medications.

01

Medical Treatment

Nicotine Replacement (NRT)

Patches, gum, lozenges, nasal spray, and inhalers

Non-Nicotine Medications

Varenicline (Chantix) and Bupropion (Zyban)

02

Behavioral Support

  • Individual or group counseling
  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)
  • Quitlines and mobile apps
  • Family and social support
03

Lifestyle Strategies

  • Avoid smoking triggers
  • Use healthy substitutes (gum, water, snacks)
  • Regular exercise
  • Stress management techniques
04

Relapse Management

  • Withdrawal symptoms peak within 3–7 days
  • Cravings gradually decrease over time
  • Relapse is common — don't give up
  • Repeated quit attempts improve success
